Student Performance Analysis

This project investigates whether being in a romantic relationship is associated with differences in academic performance among high school students. Using the UCI Student Performance dataset which includes demographic, behavioral, and academic variables. The analysis focuses on the relationship between the romantic feature and final grade outcomes (G3). This question is relevant because romantic involvement may influence time management, stress levels, and study habits, potentially affecting academic achievement (positively, negatively, or not at all).

Feature Engineering

Engineered the feature "romantic_encoded" to take all of the "yes" values from the romantic feature. This feature will then be used for the linear regression model.

In[6]:
data['romantic_encoded'] = (data['romantic'] == 'yes').astype(int)

feature_cols = [col for col in data.columns if col not in ['G3', 'romantic'] and data[col].dtype in ['int64', 'float64']]

Model Evaluation

Model performance was assessed using RMSE, MAE, and R metrics on both the training and test sets. Five-fold cross-validation was conducted to evaluate the model's consistency across different subsets of the data. Additionally, an Actual vs. Predicted plot and a residual plot were generated to visualize the model's accuracy and error distribution. These evaluation steps helped determine how effectively the model captured variation in academic performance.

In[8]:
print("Model Performance:")
print()

# Predictions
y_pred_train = model_lr.predict(X_train_scaled)
y_pred_test = model_lr.predict(X_test_scaled)

train_rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_train, y_pred_train))

Conclusions

Based on the analysis of the UCI Student Performance dataset, romantic relationship status shows very little to no meaningful impact on students' final academic performance. Although small differences appear in the descriptive statistics and visualizations, these differences are minor and not strong enough to suggest a significant relationship. In the regression model, the encoded romantic relationship variable has a small coefficient relative to other predictors, indicating that it contributes very little to explaining variation in final grades. Factors such as prior grades, absences, and academic history were far more influential predictors. Therefore, the data suggests that being in a romantic relationship does not substantially affect academic performance for students in this dataset.

Ethical Concerns/Limitations

It is important to note that the conclusions made in this project should be taken with a heavy grain of salt. First of all, this project only determines a correlation between the two factors that are being explored which are romantic relationships and how they might be correlated to students' academic performance. However, it is important to remember that correlation does not imply a causation. Additionally, there are many other factors at play when it comes to relationships that simply are not refelcted by the data. Also, relationships differ from person to person. As such, this project has given insights on how to two might be correlated, but by no means does it suggest a causation of any kind.
